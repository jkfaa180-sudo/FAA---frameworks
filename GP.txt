3. Topic-Level Weightage Methodology (Analytical Inference)
The official syllabus assigns marks only at the subject level.
It does not assign marks to individual topics.
Therefore every topic-level weightage shown later is an analytical apportionment, derived using five factors:
1. Conceptual complexity
2. Number of prerequisite concepts
3. Practice intensity required
4. Breadth within the subject
5. Share of the subject's official marks
No topic-level weightage is official.

Timeline Justification
After assessing the syllabus against the stated objective—maximum examination performance rather than textbook-level proficiency—the workload was reduced while preserving prerequisite integrity, sufficient problem-solving practice, and continuous spaced revision.
The workload is approximately 340 hours, translating to 68–85 days depending on daily study time.
Although the working assumption considered a one-month horizon, one month is not realistically sufficient for a complete beginner. The limiting factor is not the syllabus size but the sequential dependency within Accountancy (Accounting Equation → Double Entry → Journal → Ledger → Trial Balance → Final Accounts → Partnership → Cost Accounting), which cannot be compressed substantially without increasing the risk of poor retention and weaker exam performance.

Impact of Negative Marking
The presence of 0.25 negative marking does not materially increase the estimated study hours because the syllabus scope remains unchanged. However, it changes the preparation emphasis. Practice should focus on improving both accuracy and decision-making under exam conditions. Mock tests should measure not only raw score but also accuracy percentage, error patterns, and question-attempt strategy.

Assumptions
1. Subject-level marks are taken directly from the official syllabus. 
2. Topic-level weightage is analytically inferred and is not official.
3. The examination carries a negative marking of 0.25 marks for every incorrect answer. This influences the later practice and mock-test strategy by emphasizing accuracy and informed question selection rather than maximizing attempts.
4. Study-hour estimates are designed for a complete beginner with no accounting background.
5. Revision allocation follows established learning-science principles rather than syllabus instructions.

Stage 1 Summary
The analysis estimates a total preparation workload of approximately 340 hours, representing the leanest responsible preparation for a beginner targeting maximum performance in the JKSSB Finance Accounts Assistant examination. The preparation duration emerges naturally from this workload, requiring 68–85 days depending on whether 5, 4.5, or 4 hours are studied daily. The strategy prioritizes Accountancy and General Knowledge due to their official weightage and dependency structure while ensuring Tier 1 remains a self-contained, exam-ready plan if the available preparation time is unexpectedly reduced. Because the examination includes 0.25 negative marking per incorrect answer, later stages will prioritize accuracy, error reduction, and intelligent attempt strategy alongside syllabus coverage.
Stage 2 — Roadmap System
Planning Philosophy
This roadmap is designed around one objective:
Reach the highest possible exam score within the available study hours, not textbook mastery.
Every decision follows four principles:
1. Learn concepts only after their prerequisites.
2. Minimize forgetting through continuous revision.
3. Spend most time where marks and dependency are highest.
4. Maximize net score, not attempts, because of negative marking.

───

Why this roadmap is NOT in syllabus order
The official syllabus is a content checklist, not a recommended learning sequence.
This roadmap instead follows the dependency relationships identified in Stage 1.
Example:
Official order
Accountancy → English → Statistics → Mathematics...
Roadmap order
Accounting Foundations → Core Accounting → Economics → Statistics → Mathematics → Remaining Subjects
because
• Later accounting topics require earlier accounting topics.
• Economics introduces financial terminology used throughout Accountancy.
• Statistics becomes easier after basic Probability.
• Mathematics progresses from simpler algebra to higher-level concepts.
• Memory-heavy subjects are delayed until conceptual workload decreases.

───

Alternative Learning Orders Considered
Option 1 — Strict Syllabus Order
Advantages
• Simple to follow.
• Mirrors the official document.
Disadvantages
• Ignores prerequisite relationships.
• Frequent context switching.
• Lower retention.
• Difficult for beginners.

───

Option 2 — Weightage First
(Accountancy → GK → Remaining Subjects)
Advantages
• Covers highest-mark subjects first.
Disadvantages
• Extremely mentally demanding.
• Delays easy scoring subjects.
• Large revision burden later.
• Increased forgetting.

───

Selected Strategy — Dependency First + Parallel Learning
Advantages
• Strong conceptual foundation.
• Better long-term retention.
• Lower cognitive load.
• Easier revision.
• Continuous exposure to factual subjects.
• Better suited for beginners.
This is therefore the preferred roadmap.

Retrieval Practice

Spaced Repetition

Topics learned earliest receive more revision than topics learned recently.

Stage 2 Summary
This roadmap combines dependency-driven sequencing, parallel-subject study, continuous revision, and accuracy-focused practice into a single preparation system. Rather than completing one subject before moving to another, it uses Primary, Secondary, and Light study blocks to balance deep conceptual learning with regular exposure to factual subjects, reducing forgetting without excessive context switching. The later phases progressively shift the focus from learning to retrieval, then to exam simulation and targeted improvement, with mock-test analysis centered on net score and accuracy because of the 0.25 negative marking. The result is a roadmap that remains effective across the full 68–85 day preparation window while still allowing a Tier 1 fallback if the available time is reduced.
