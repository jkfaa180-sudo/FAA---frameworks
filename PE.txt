• Foundational literacy: Tenses, articles, modals, prepositions → sentence correction/jumbled sentences → narration, voice, clauses → cloze, idioms, vocabulary.
• Numeracy core: linear equations → set theory → relations and functions → matrices and determinants → permutations and combinations → probability → coordinate geometry → vectors → limits → derivatives.
• Statistics: data collection and questionnaires → tabulation/compilation → central tendency → index numbers → probability/attributes → demography and vital statistics.
• Accountancy: terminology and double entry → accounting equation → journal → voucher approach → ledger → cash book → bank reconciliation → trial balance → trading account → profit & loss account → balance sheet → partnership accounts → financial statements/analysis → cost accounting and budgetary control → audit/PFMS/IFMS/social accounting/taxation/developments.
• Computers: fundamentals and input/output → hardware/software → operating system → MS Word/Excel/Access/PowerPoint/PDF/Internet/Email → virus/antivirus → open source → IT in governance.
• Economics: intro to economics → demand analysis → consumer behaviour and indifference curves → production and factors → factor pricing → market structures → national income → growth and developing economy problems → planning vs market → reforms → fiscal policy → monetary policy → RBI.
• General Knowledge: Indian polity/geography/history/culture/heritage → demography/census → transport and communication → environment/ecology → J&K history → J&K geography → J&K economy → J&K culture → J&K tourism → J&K Reorganisation Act → current affairs maintained daily from the start and compiled late.

Summary
The revised dependency map is tighter than the original because it builds accounting in a strict prerequisite chain, integrates Excel earlier, and treats current affairs as continuous maintenance rather than a late-only block [1]. Sequencing now follows learning dependencies first, with subject weight used to tune time allocation rather than dictate order.

Revision Strategy
• Use daily spaced recall from day 2 onward: yesterday’s accounting steps, one math rule block, one English rule block, and one GK recall block [1].
• Use interleaving only after the core bookkeeping chain is stable: alternate bookkeeping with math/statistics and a small English/GK block to improve discrimination and retention.
• Keep an error log from the first week; every recurring mistake should be revisited after 1 day, 3 days, and 7 days.
• Use Feynman-style self-explanation for accounting and economics concepts, because those subjects are more definition-sensitive than formula-heavy.

Practice Strategy
• Deliberate practice is front-loaded on journal/ledger/trial balance and final accounts because those are the highest-leverage objective areas in the syllabus [1].
• For statistics and mathematics, progress from guided worked examples to independent items, then timed mixed sets.
• For English, start with rule recognition, move to transformation questions, then cloze and sentence rearrangement.
• For GK and science, use retrieval practice rather than rereading; the score comes from recall speed, not depth.

Mock Test Strategy
• Start sectional tests after Core Learning I, not before, because the accounting base must exist before timed mixed practice makes sense.
• Increase frequency only after the first full revision, because early full mocks would mostly measure incomplete learning rather than test readiness.
• Review every mock by category: concept gap, formula/format gap, careless error, or time-management error.
• Keep the final two mocks as near-real simulations with strict timing and no interruption.

Milestones
• Milestone 1: accounting terminology, double entry, equation, and journal are usable without notes [1].
• Milestone 2: ledger, cash book, bank reconciliation, and trial balance are executable from a transaction set [1].
• Milestone 3: trading account, P&L, and balance sheet are prepared from adjusted figures with acceptable accuracy [1].
• Milestone 4: core mathematics and statistics question types are solvable in timed conditions [1].
• Milestone 5: English grammar and computer basics produce quick marks with low error rates [1].
• Milestone 6: GK J&K block is consolidated enough to answer direct factual questions quickly [1].

Risk Assessment
• Risk: Spending too long on low-yield accounting tail topics. Control: cap the tail until Tier 1 accuracy is stable.
• Risk: Probability/combinatorics becoming a bottleneck in mathematics and statistics. Control: teach P&C before probability and use daily mixed drills.
• Risk: GK becoming passive reading. Control: force recall in short bursts and keep current affairs daily.
• Risk: Overconfidence from English and computers because they look easy. Control: maintain them through short timed drills, not full study blocks.

Sequencing Rationale
The dependency-driven order is stronger than a strict weightage-first order because it prevents premature exposure to journal/ledger/final-account tasks before the learner has the accounting vocabulary and structure needed to understand them [1]. A weightage-first sequence would put J&K GK and broad factual memorization too early, but that creates more review burden and does not help the beginner build the core accounting chain. The chosen order also improves retention by grouping prerequisites tightly and using interleaving only after the core chain is stable.

Tier structure inside the roadmap
• Tier 1 topics dominate Foundation, Core Learning I, and the first half of Core Learning II.
• Tier 2 topics are added in Consolidation and later phases, so the plan can be compressed if time shortens without breaking the accounting core.
• If the timeline is shortened, the minimum coherent exam-ready subplan is the full accounting chain up to balance sheet, arithmetic and core algebra/probability, English grammar essentials, computer basics, and high-yield GK maintenance.

Stress test summary
The roadmap stays coherent if one study week is lost because the revision and mock blocks are already separated and can absorb compression [1]. If one major topic takes longer, the plan can steal time from Tier 2 tails without breaking the core chain. If the student learns faster, the extra time flows naturally into more PYQs and mock analysis. If the exam is confirmed sooner than the working horizon, Tier 1 still stands on its own as a complete subplan.
