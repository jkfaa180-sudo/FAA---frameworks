Sequencing Justification

Why the dependency‑driven order (Phase 1→2→3) over a strictly weightage‑first order?

A weightage‑first approach would front‑load the three 30‑mark subjects (GK, Accounting) in their entirety before touching lighter subjects. That would mean covering the entire GK J&K block and the advanced Accounting tail (Social Audit, PFMS, etc.) before studying any Statistics or Computers. Credible alternative, but three flaws make our chosen order superior:

1. Prerequisite chains: Accounting is sequentially gated. You cannot understand Trial Balance without Ledger, or BRS without Cash Book. Forcing all 30 marks of Accounting early means teaching complex topics like Partnership before the student is fluent in Final Accounts — a cognitive overload that hurts retention and performance.
2. Conceptual fatigue without interleaving: Weightage‑first would load 10+ days of nothing but Accounting and GK, leading to diminishing returns. Interleaving lighter conceptual subjects (Science, Computers) in Phase 2 provides recovery and better long‑term retention through context switching, a well‑established learning‑science finding.
3. Revision scheduling: In our order, the Accounting core (highest practice‑need) finishes by Day 21, giving it a full 24 days of spaced revision before the exam. A weightage‑first order would compress that revision window, weakening high‑weightage topics right when they matter most.

Thus, the dependency‑driven interleaved sequence respects cognitive architecture while still completing Tier 1 earlier and enabling robust revision.

Revision Strategy (Continuous, not bolted‑on)

• Daily 15‑min “lightning revision” : Each day begins with 5‑10 flashcards from previous days’ topics (spaced retrieval). After Phase 1, this expands to 30‑min cross‑subject quiz.
• Weekly cumulative reviews: Every 7th day is a lighter day dedicated to a 2‑hour systematic review of that week’s Tier 1 topics through mind‑map reconstruction and error‑log re‑solving (Phase 2 onwards).
• Phase‑end formal revision: Phases 3, 4, and 6 contain dedicated revision blocks, as outlined.
• Interleaved problem sets: From Phase 2 onward, practice sets mix at least 2 subjects (e.g., a study session of 40 min Accounting + 20 min Math problems), enforcing discriminative retrieval (recognising which concept to apply).

Spaced repetition intervals: new fact/concept revisited after 1 day, 3 days, 7 days, 14 days, and then as needed. Flashcards (physical or app) track this.

───

Practice Strategy

• Progressive difficulty:Level 1: Single‑concept drills (e.g., “journalise these 10 capital‑related transactions”).
Level 2: Multi‑concept mixed problems (e.g., “pass journal entries, post to ledger, and prepare trial balance from a list of 20 items”).
Level 3: Exam‑style application with adjustments and time pressure (from Phase 4 onward).
• Error log maintenance: Every incorrect question is logged with:
 — Subject, Topic, Mistake type, Root cause, Correct solution, Date.
 Re‑attempted after 2 days; if still wrong, flagged for Deliberate Practice session.
• Quantitative subjects: minimum question targets are set per topic (from Stage 1 practice hours) and tracked in a progress sheet.
• Theory subjects: practice means self‑quizzing from notes (retrieval), not re‑reading. A list of “Questions I should be able to answer” is prepared for each GK/Economics section.

───

Mock Test Strategy

• Number of mocks: 4 full‑length tests (included in Stage 1 hour total). Early mocks are not advisable because the syllabus is incomplete; starting at Phase 5 ensures all content is covered and revision has begun.
• Simulation fidelity: Same time slot (2 hours uninterrupted), OMR sheet if possible, no breaks, no external aids. Psychologically mimics exam day.
• Post‑mock analysis: 2 hours per mock spent not on re‑reading but on error taxonomy and creating micro‑remediation tasks.
• Escalating difficulty: Mock 1 and 2 can be slightly easier or equal to expected exam standard; Mock 3 and 4 are at/slightly above expected difficulty to build composure under pressure.

Risk Assessment & Mitigation


Risk
Mitigation (built into the plan)

Student misses one week of study
Tier 1 labelling allows immediate compression: drop all Medium/Low topics from remaining phases, slash mocks to 2, and proceed with the core. The 45‑day plan can shrink to ~30 days by eliminating Phase 3 partially and merging Phases 4‑6.

One major topic takes substantially more time (e.g., Partnership Accounts)
The Phase 2 duration has a buffer: 14 days is slightly over the cumulative Tier 1 topic hours to absorb a 1‑2 day overrun. If still exceeded, the topic is treated as “critical weak” post‑Phase 2 and addressed intensively in Phase 3 revision instead of new Tier 2 learning.

Student progresses faster than expected (completes tasks in 75% of time)
Extra time is not “free” – it is reinvested into additional mixed practice and deeper PYQ sets. The plan never actively stretches to fill time; the calendar will have “bonus practice” slots.

Exam confirmed for a horizon meaningfully shorter than 1 month (e.g., 15 days)
The Tier 1‑only sub‑plan is activated: Foundation and Accounting core compressed into first 8 days, rest of Tier 1 by Day 15, 1 mock on Day 16, final revision Day 17‑18. It is structurally coherent because all Tier 1 dependencies map onto the same chain; Tier 2 is discarded entirely.



Stress‑test result: The plan passes all four scenarios. Tier 1 alone holds as a complete, exam‑ready sub‑plan. No structural weakness found.
