6. Study Strategy: Parallel/Interleaved, Not Sequential — decided here, built out in Stage 2/3

You asked me to decide whether to complete one subject before moving to the next, or run 2–3 in parallel — and which stage that belongs in.

Decision: parallel/interleaved, not sequential. This isn't a close call — running subjects fully sequentially would actively contradict both learning science and §2's own dependency map:

• Retention: if you finish a subject on Day 5 and don't touch it again until Day 24, the forgetting curve has already done most of its damage by exam day. Interleaving with periodic revisits is the single best-evidenced fix for this — it's why spaced repetition and interleaving are both on your own brief's required-methods list.
• Structural necessity: §2 already established that Accountancy (dependency chain) and GK (current-affairs timing) can't be done as isolated blocks anyway — Accountancy needs to run continuously to keep each topic's prerequisite fresh for the next, and Current Affairs deliberately needs to run late, not early. A sequential "finish subject A, then B" structure can't accommodate either of these.
• Cognitive load balancing: pairing one effortful subject (Accountancy) with one lighter, more recall-based partner subject each day is easier to sustain at 5.2h/day than two effortful subjects back to back, or one subject for 5 hours straight.

The model: Accountancy as a daily anchor (~2.5–3h/day, every day, following its dependency order) + one rotating partner subject per day (~2–2.5h, cycling through GK/English/Stats/Math/Economics/Science/Computers) + short (~20–30 min) spaced-revision touches every 2–3 days revisiting whatever was covered several days back. That's "2 subjects in parallel" most days, occasionally "3" when a revision touch layers in.

Where this lives: the decision is recorded here; the actual phase intensities and rotation order are Stage 2's job (its "Phase-wise Roadmap" objective/intensity columns), and the literal day-by-day pairing (which subject partners with Accountancy on Day 7 specifically) is Stage 3's job. I'll carry this structure into both.

Sequencing Logic: Why Dependency-Driven Interleaving Beats the Obvious Alternative

The obvious alternative — weightage-first sequencing: study GK and Accountancy (60 of 120 marks) exclusively for the first ~2 weeks, since they're worth the most, then split the remaining time across the six 10-mark subjects.

Why it loses to the dependency-driven, interleaved order used below:
1. It starves zero-background Economics and the now-harder Computers of runway. Six subjects squeezed into the tail end (including a subject you have zero background in) is far tighter than Stage 1's own bottom-up hour estimates allow for those subjects.
2. It's worse for retention, not better. Cramming 60 marks of content into the first weeks with no revisit before the exam is exactly the "blocked study" pattern spaced-repetition research warns against — you'd front-load the highest-value content into the position where it decays the most by exam day.
3. It ignores real prerequisite chains in Math and Statistics (Math's P&C must precede Probability in both subjects) — those chains don't disappear just because the subjects are individually low-weightage; rushing them late raises error rates exactly where formula-based, learnable marks should be easiest to bank.

The dependency-driven interleaved order avoids all three by running Accountancy and GK continuously from day 1 (the two subjects that structurally can't be isolated blocks anyway) while threading the other six through in dependency-respecting order.

Revision Strategy

• Continuous, not bolted on: every Phase 2 topic carries an embedded spaced-revision touch (Stage 1's per-topic "Rev h" column) roughly every 2–3 days, not a single end-of-syllabus pass.
• Spaced repetition in practice: the partner-subject rotation in Phase 2 is sequenced so that no subject goes untouched for more than ~5–6 days at a stretch (Accountancy, as the anchor, is touched daily).
• Retrieval practice over re-reading: Phase 3's "first revision" is explicitly active recall (blank-page, self-quiz) rather than re-reading notes — re-reading creates false familiarity without testing actual retrieval.
• Current Affairs is the deliberate exception: studied late (Phase 6), not spaced throughout, because its content is time-decaying in the opposite direction — older "current" affairs become less relevant, not more memorable.

Practice Strategy

• Progressive difficulty: Accountancy practice starts with single-concept drills (journal entries only) before compound problems (full Trading→P&L→Balance Sheet sequences), never the reverse.
• Error Analysis built in: every practice block ends with a short error-log entry (what was missed, why) — this is what Phase 4/5's "weak-area list" is built from, not a one-off mock debrief.
• Deliberate Practice on the highest-difficulty clusters: Partnership Accounts and Bank Reconciliation get isolated, repeated drilling (not just embedded in general Accountancy practice) given their Stage-1 "High difficulty/High practice intensity" rating.
• Feynman Technique for conceptual subjects: Economics and the Accountancy conceptual tail (PFMS/IFMS/Cost concepts) are checked via "explain it in plain language, out loud or in writing" rather than recognition-only review, since MCQ recognition can mask shallow understanding of these zero-background concepts.

Mock Test Strategy

• Increasing frequency and realism: Day 29's pure active-recall consolidation → 3 full-length, fully-timed mocks (Days 30–32), satisfying the prompt's "frequency increases as exam approaches" requirement. (Revised: the half-length diagnostic mock originally planned for Phase 3 was dropped when the calendar compressed — Day 30's Mock 1 + PYQ session now does that diagnostic job.)
• 0.25 negative marking factored in from the first mock: practice the actual exam's risk calculus (when to skip vs. guess) rather than learning content and guessing strategy separately.
• Every mock is immediately followed by review, not deferred — confirmed in Stage 3: mock review time runs close to mock sitting time, given the harder statement-based question style needs more careful post-mortem.
