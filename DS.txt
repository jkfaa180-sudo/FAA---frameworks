1. Recommended Preparation Duration & Timeline Justification

Total study hours: 125 hours (unchanged bottom‑up sum).
Daily capacity: 4.5 hours/day (midpoint of 4–5).
Preparation duration = 125 ÷ 4.5 ≈ 27.8 days → 28 days (4 weeks).

This remains the leanest responsible estimate that covers every syllabus topic with adequate theory, minimal essential practice, and continuous revision for a true beginner. It fits within the 1‑month working horizon per the Exam Timeline Assumption. Should the exam be confirmed for a later date, the extra time is available for deeper practice and additional mock tests; if it is sooner, the Tier 1 subset (see Priority Matrix) can be delivered in roughly 3 weeks (~95 hours) and still forms a coherent, exam‑ready sub‑plan.

Major Sequencing Decisions — Rationale and Alternatives

Before presenting the phase‑wise roadmap, this section justifies the subject‑level learning sequences adopted from the Stage 1 Dependency Map. For each major departure from the official syllabus order or from a simple weightage‑first approach, a credible alternative is weighed and the chosen order is confirmed as superior for a complete beginner.

───

1. Accountancy & Book Keeping

Official syllabus order (excerpt): Introduction → Accounting Equation & Journal → Voucher Approach → Bank Reconciliation → Cash Book → Financial Audit → Financial Statements → Partnership → Ledger → Elements of Double Entry → Trial Balance → Trading Account → P&L/Balance Sheet → Social Accounting → …
This order scatters core procedural topics (Ledger after BRS, Trial Balance before Trading Account) and places Bank Reconciliation before Cash Book, which introduces BRS without the cash‑book concept it depends on.

Chosen dependency‑driven order:
Introduction & Double Entry → Journal → Voucher → Ledger → Cash Book → Bank Reconciliation → Trial Balance → Trading Account → P&L/Balance Sheet → Partnership Accounts → (then conceptual tail: Financial Management, Cost Accounting, Audit, Social Accounting, PFMS, Taxation).

· BRS follows Cash Book immediately — while the cash‑book logic is fresh, the two topics reinforce each other.
· Ledger is taught early so that posting from journal becomes automatic.
· Trial Balance, Trading A/c, and Final Accounts are mastered in strict sequence before tackling the more complex Partnership Accounts.

Alternative weighed — weightage‑first order: Start with highest‑weight topics first: Partnership → Final Accounts → Journal.

· Why rejected: A beginner cannot handle partnership adjustments without first understanding journal entries, ledger posting, and final‑account preparation. This would create constant backtracking, confusion, and a fragile knowledge base. The dependency‑driven order builds the procedural spine first, after which high‑weightage topics are learned faster and retained more securely.

───

1. General Economics

Official syllabus order: Introduction → Fiscal & Monetary Policy → Indifference Curve → Demand Analysis → Factor Pricing → Marginal Productivity → Ricardian Rent → Market Pricing → Factors of Production → Developing Economy → Planning vs Market → Economic Reforms → National Income → Economic Growth → RBI.
This order jumps from fiscal policy (macro) back to micro demand theory, then to factor pricing, then to national income — with no pedagogical flow.

Chosen micro‑to‑macro order:
Introduction → Demand Analysis → Consumer Behaviour (Indifference Curve) → Factors & Production Laws → Factor Pricing (Marginal Productivity, Ricardian Rent) → Market Structures → National Income → Economic Growth → Developing Economy → Planning vs Market → Economic Reforms → Fiscal & Monetary Policy → RBI.

· The student builds a mental model of individual economic behaviour first, then firm and market behaviour, before aggregating to national income and policy.
· Fiscal & Monetary Policy and RBI are placed last because they require an understanding of national income and macroeconomic goals, which are now firmly in place.

Alternative weighed — policy‑first order (weightage): Start with Fiscal/Monetary Policy and RBI because they carry high marks.

· Why rejected: Policy instruments (CRR, SLR, repo rate) are meaningless without the context of money supply, inflation, and national income. A beginner would memorise terms without comprehension, severely hurting application‑type MCQs.

───

1. Knowledge of Computers

Official syllabus order: Basic Applications → Fundamentals → Open Source → Input/Output → OS → MS Word/Excel/Access/PPT/PDF → Internet → Virus → Hardware/Software → Processing System (CPU, Peripherals, Storage, Firmware, Humanware) → IT in Governance.
The list jumps between hardware, software, and applications without a layering logic.

Chosen hardware‑to‑software build‑up:
Fundamentals → Input/Output Devices → Hardware & Software → Operating System → MS Office/PDF → Internet & Email → Virus & Anti‑virus → Open Source → Processing System details → IT in Governance.

· The student first understands what a computer is (fundamentals, I/O, hardware/software), then how the OS manages it, then how application software runs, and finally how networks and security extend it.
· MS Office is taught after the OS concept, mirroring real‑world usage.

Alternative weighed — application‑first order: Start with MS Office and Internet (high‑weight, familiar).

· Why rejected: Without the hardware/OS fundamentals, the student cannot answer questions about file formats, OS‑specific shortcuts, or the difference between system and application software. A foundation‑first approach yields higher accuracy across all MCQs, not just the MS Office ones.

───

1. General Knowledge — Current Affairs

Common coaching approach: Study static GK first, then cram Current Affairs in the final week.

Chosen approach: Daily 20‑min Current Affairs thread from Day 1, alongside static topics.

· Spaced retrieval of news events over 4 weeks builds durable memory, whereas a final‑week cram risks catastrophic forgetting under exam pressure.
· This also allows the student to connect static GK (e.g., J&K economy) with recent news, deepening both.

Alternative weighed — final‑week Current Affairs sprint.

· Why rejected: The JKSSB FAA exam can ask 4–5 Current Affairs questions covering multiple months; a single‑week sprint is simply insufficient for a beginner with zero prior habit. Daily low‑dose exposure is more efficient and less stressful.

Phase‑wise Roadmap

Every phase lists which topics are Tier 1 (Critical + High) and Tier 2 (Medium + Low) as per the Stage 1 Priority Matrix. This allows clean compression: if the exam is suddenly confirmed for a shorter horizon, omit all Tier 2 topics and reduce revision/mock intensity accordingly.

───

Phase 1: Foundation (Days 1–4 | ~18 h)

Objective: Build the non‑negotiable procedural core of Accountancy (Double Entry through Journal) and initiate daily retrieval habits for GK, English vocab, and Current Affairs. Introduce the lightest theory subjects to begin spaced repetition early without overloading the accounting learning curve.

Topics covered:

· Accountancy (Tier 1): Introduction to Financial Accounting & its terms; Elements of Double Entry Book Keeping; Accounting Equation and Journal; Voucher Approach in Accounting.
· General English (Tier 1 start): Tenses (theory + error‑spotting drills).
· General Knowledge (Tier 1 start): Current Affairs (daily 20 min thread begins); Political & Physical Divisions of India (first half); J&K Reorganisation Act, 2019.
· General Science (Tier 1 start): Newton’s Laws of Motion (basic concepts, no numerical practice).
· Knowledge of Computers (Tier 1 start): Fundamentals of Computer Science; Input & Output Devices.

Note: Tier 2 topics are not introduced in this phase — the cognitive load of learning double‑entry journalising from zero leaves no room for additional heavy theory.

Intensity & daily structure:

· Morning block (2 h): Accountancy — theory followed by immediate practice (classifying accounts, passing simple journal entries). Begin an error log for every incorrect journal entry.
· Midday block (1.5 h): English Tenses (rule study + 20 MCQs), then GK static reading with map‑based active recall (Political Divisions).
· Evening block (1 h): Computers or Science alternation + Current Affairs (summarise 3–4 news items in 2 sentences each from a compiled digest; no browsing). End with 15 min review of the day’s error log.

Learning methods used:

· Deliberate Practice: Journal entries are deliberately drilled with progressive difficulty — first simple cash/credit transactions, then compound entries.
· Active Recall: Map‑based recall for Political Divisions (close eyes, mentally name states/UTs and their capitals).
· Error Analysis: Error log started on Day 1 and reviewed daily.

Deliverables:

· 50+ journal entries passed correctly (at least 90% accuracy).
· 1‑page condensed note: Double‑entry rules, account classification chart.
· Error log with initial patterns categorised (e.g., “expense vs asset confusion”).

Exit criteria:

· The student can, within 10 minutes, correctly classify 20 given transactions into personal/real/nominal and pass the corresponding journal entry without referring to notes.
· Tenses: 80% accuracy on 25 mixed fill‑in‑the‑blank MCQs.

───

Phase 2: Core Learning (Days 5–14 | 10 days, ~45 h)

Objective: Complete the procedural Accountancy chain (Ledger → Final Accounts + Partnership), finish all Tier 1 theory across subjects, and introduce Tier 2 topics where they interleave naturally. Establish continuous interleaved revision of Phase 1 topics.

Topics covered (Tier 1 unless marked Tier 2):

Accountancy: Ledger Accounts; Cash Book; Bank Reconciliation Statement; Trial Balance; Trading Account; Profit & Loss A/c and Balance Sheet; Partnership Accounts (basics); Taxation & Tax Laws (Tier 1).

· Tier 2 tail begins in the last 2 days of this phase: Financial Management/Statements; Cost Accounting; Cost Management – Budgetary Control.

General English: Active & Passive Voice; Articles; Prepositions; Modals; Narration; Clauses; Comprehension (blanks); Synonyms & Antonyms; Idioms & Phrases.

· Tier 2: Jumbled Sentences; Pairs of Words.

General Knowledge: Indian Culture, Heritage & Freedom Struggle; Rivers & Lakes; Weather, Climate, Crops; Environment, Ecology & Biodiversity; Demography – Census; J&K History, Economy, Geography, Heritage, Tourist Destinations.

· Tier 2: Transport & Communication.

Statistics: Data Collection/Questionnaires/Tabulation; Measures of Central Tendency; Demography–Census (stats angle); Theory of Probability; Theory of Index Numbers (WPI & CPI); Vital Statistics, Fertility & Mortality.

· Tier 2: Theory of Attributes.

Mathematics: Linear Equations; Set Theory; Relations & Functions; Matrices & Determinants; Permutations & Combinations; Binomial Theorem; Probability; Simple & Compound Interest.

· Tier 2: Limits & Derivatives; Coordinate Geometry; 3D Geometry; Vectors.

General Economics: Introduction; Demand Analysis; Indifference Curve; Factors of Production; Factor Pricing; Marginal Productivity; Ricardian Rent; Market Structures; National Income; Economic Growth; Developing Economy; Planning vs Market; Economic Reforms; Fiscal & Monetary Policy; Role of RBI (all Tier 1 except Indifference Curve, Ricardian Rent, Developing Economy, Planning vs Market — these are Tier 2 but introduced here because the micro‑to‑macro flow includes them).

General Science: Electric Current & Energy; Chemical Equations; Metals & Non‑metals; Sources of Energy; Nutrition; Respiration; Transportation in Plants; Diseases; Vitamins; Ecosystem & Food Chains; Pollution; Ozone; Greenhouse (all Tier 1 except Ozone/Greenhouse — Tier 2 but included for environment cluster completeness).

Knowledge of Computers: Hardware & Software; Operating System; MS Word, Excel, Access, PowerPoint, PDF; Internet & Email (Tier 1). Tier 2: Virus & Anti‑virus; Open Source; Processing System details; IT in Governance.

Daily structure (4.5 h):

· Accounting block (2 h): One major procedural topic per 1–2 days, with 50% time for problem‑solving.
· Interleaved subject block (1.5 h): Rotating mix of Economics, Statistics, Mathematics, and Computers — never the same subject on consecutive days, to force interleaving and improve discrimination.
· GK & English thread (1 h): 20 min Current Affairs; 20 min GK static (active recall from earlier notes); 20 min English practice (mixed grammar MCQs).
· End‑of‑day review (15 min): Error log review + self‑explanation (Feynman technique) of one accounting concept.

Practice strategy during Core:

· Progressive difficulty: After learning BRS, practice moves from simple timing differences to overdraft‑based reconciliations.
· Spaced repetition: A 30‑min “Review of the Week’s Topics” slot is fixed every Saturday (Day 7, 14) where the student retrieves all topics studied that week, without notes.

Deliverables:

· Completed error log with at least 50 entries across accounting and math.
· One A4‑sheet condensed notes per GK topic (mind‑map style).
· 100+ grammar MCQs solved.
· Self‑recorded 2‑minute Feynman explanation of: “How a transaction flows from Journal to Balance Sheet.”

Exit criteria:

· BRS: 90% accuracy on 10 MCQs without hints.
· Final Accounts: Correctly prepare a simple Trading and P&L A/c from a given Trial Balance with at most one adjusting entry.
· Partnership: Pass basic admission journal entries with 80% accuracy.
· A mixed‑subject MCQ test of 50 questions (from all subjects covered so far) yields ≥ 60%.

───

Phase 3: Consolidation (Days 15–20 | 6 days, ~27 h)

Objective: Intensify practice on high‑weight Tier 1 topics, fill residual Tier 2 gaps, and begin full‑subject cumulative retrieval. First contact with previous‑year questions (PYQs) as untimed practice.

Key activities:

· Accountancy practice marathon: Mixed journal/BRS/Final Accounts problems daily (1 h). Emphasis on error analysis — every mistake is logged and a corrected version is written out.
· Subject‑wise consolidation quizzes: Each day, one hour is dedicated to a different subject’s Tier 1 MCQs (e.g., Day 15: Economics 30 Q; Day 16: Statistics 25 Q; Day 17: Science 30 Q; Day 18: Computers 30 Q; Day 19: Math 25 Q; Day 20: English 30 Q).
· Tier 2 completion: Remaining Tier 2 topics that were skipped in Core are learned via rapid 30‑min theory bursts followed by 20‑min question practice (e.g., Financial Audit, Social Accounting, PFMS, Theory of Attributes, Limits & Derivatives).
· GK revision intensified: Daily 30‑min active recall of J&K topics using flashcards; current affairs revision from a compiled monthly digest.

Learning methods used:

· Retrieval Practice: Subject quizzes are closed‑book, with answers checked only after completion.
· Interleaving: Practice sets mix questions from different subjects within the same session to simulate exam randomness.
· Deliberate Practice: Only the weakest sub‑skills (e.g., adjusting entries in Final Accounts, voice transformation errors) are targeted in extra drills.

Deliverables:

· Error log updated and patterns summarised (e.g., “Top 3 recurring mistakes”).
· First full‑length untimed PYQ paper (2022 or equivalent) attempted and scored.
· Flashcards for all GK topics ready.

Exit criteria:

· Untimed PYQ score ≥ 65%.
· Accountancy mixed‑problem set of 30 questions: ≥ 80% accuracy.
· All Tier 2 topic readings complete with at least one short MCQ quiz per topic.

───

Phase 4: First Revision (Days 21–23 | 3 days, ~13.5 h)

Objective: Systematic full‑syllabus sweep using active recall and spaced repetition, prioritising Tier 1. Identify and list the top 3 weak areas for remediation.

Structure:

· Day 21: Accountancy (all topics, rapid recall of rules and formats) + English (full grammar drill).
· Day 22: GK & Science (mind‑map review, map pointing, vitamin/disease pairings) + Computers (OS and MS Office focus).
· Day 23: Economics & Statistics & Mathematics (formula sheet creation from memory, then check; mixed numerical problem set).

Each day includes a 30‑min “blank‑page test”: write everything you remember about a given topic (e.g., “National Income”) from memory, then fill gaps using notes — a pure retrieval practice technique.

Exit criteria:

· Blank‑page tests show at least 70% completeness for Tier 1 topics.
· A 100‑question mixed mock (untimed) scored ≥ 65%.

───

Phase 5: PYQs & Mock Tests (Days 24–26 | 3 days, ~13.5 h)

Objective: Simulate exam conditions, build time management, and analyse performance.

Schedule:

· Day 24: Timed Mock Test 1 (120 Q, 2 h) in the morning, followed by detailed analysis (1.5 h) — categorise errors by subject and type (conceptual gap, careless, time pressure).
· Day 25: Timed Mock Test 2, same analysis routine. Focus remediation on the top 2 error categories.
· Day 26: One untimed PYQ paper (different year) + rapid review of all error logs from mocks.

Mock test strategy:

· Tests are taken under strict exam conditions — no phone, no breaks.
· The first mock is intentionally harder to build resilience; the second matches expected exam difficulty.
· Progressive difficulty in mocks: Mock 1 has more conceptual traps; Mock 2 mirrors standard objective‑type pattern.

Exit criteria:

· Timed mock score ≥ 70% (84/120) on Mock 2.
· Completion of paper within 1 h 55 min, with at least 5 min for revision.

───

Phase 6: Weak‑Area Improvement (Day 27 | 1 day, ~4.5 h)

Objective: Close the highest‑impact gaps identified in mock analysis without reopening the entire syllabus.

Process:

· Morning (2 h): Target the single weakest subject area (likely Accountancy adjustments or Statistics numerical) with focused deliberate practice (10–15 targeted questions).
· Midday (1.5 h): Review the error log of the entire preparation — rewrite the correct logic for the 10 most stubborn mistakes.
· Evening (1 h): Light revision of high‑confidence Tier 1 topics (e.g., Current Affairs highlights, English prepositions, Computer shortcuts) to maintain freshness.

Exit criteria:

· Re‑attempt the specific question types from the weakest area and achieve ≥ 85% accuracy.

───

Phase 7: Final Revision (Day 28 | 1 day, ~4.5 h)

Objective: Consolidate confidence, reinforce key formulas and facts, and ensure mental readiness — no new learning.

Structure:

· 2 h: Rapid formula‑sheet review (Mathematics, Statistics, Accounting adjustments) + GK “top 50 facts” flashcards.
· 1 h: English error‑spotting quick drill.
· 1 h: Relaxed reading of own condensed notes and error log summaries.
· 30 min: Visualisation of exam‑day routine, positive closure.

Deliverable: A single‑page “cheat sheet” containing only the most frequently forgotten points, to be read once on exam morning.

───

Revision Strategy (Embedded & Phase‑Specific)

· Continuous revision (Days 1–20):
· Daily: 15 min error log review; 20 min current affairs retrieval.
· Weekly: Saturday cumulative quiz covering all topics of that week.
· Spaced repetition: Each topic is revisited at 1‑day, 3‑day, 7‑day, and 14‑day intervals using flashcards and a simple spreadsheet tracker (or a free SRS tool if preferred).
· Phase 4 (First Revision): Dedicated full‑sweep retrieval practice (blank‑page tests, mind‑map reconstruction).
· Phase 7 (Final Revision): Confidence‑building light review only — no new material.

───

Practice Strategy

· Progressive Difficulty: Numerical topics start with formula‑application MCQs, then move to multi‑step problems. Grammar moves from isolated fill‑in‑the‑blank to paragraph‑level error spotting.
· Deliberate Practice: Time is invested disproportionately in weak areas identified by error logs, not spread evenly.
· Interleaving: After Core phase, daily practice sets mix 2–3 subjects. Example: a 30‑Q set might contain 10 Accountancy, 10 GK, 5 English, 5 Math.
· Error Analysis: Every wrong answer is logged with (a) why it was wrong, (b) the correct concept, (c) a plan to avoid it next time. This log is the primary revision resource.

───

Mock Test Strategy

· Three full‑length mocks (Day 24, 25, and one optional earlier if time permits) under timed conditions.
· Analysis protocol: Categorise errors as Conceptual, Application, Careless, or Time‑related. Address conceptual gaps with targeted theory review; application gaps with additional practice; careless errors with a pre‑exam checklist ritual.
· Frequency: Concentrated in the final week to build exam‑day stamina without burning out earlier.
· PYQ exposure: Two untimed PYQ papers in Phases 3 and 5 provide real question patterns without the pressure of a ticking clock initially.

Risk Assessment & Stress Test

Before finalising this roadmap, four disruption scenarios were tested against the plan to ensure structural resilience.

───

Scenario A: Student misses one full week of study (e.g., Days 8–14, the second half of Core Learning).

· Mitigation built in: All Tier 1 accountancy topics (Journal, Ledger, Cash Book, BRS, Trial Balance, Final Accounts) are already introduced by Day 7. The missed week would skip Partnership, Taxation, and some Tier 2 theory.
· Recovery path: Compress Phase 3 (Consolidation) to 3 days, drop all Tier 2 topics, and use the recovered 3 days to learn Partnership Accounts and Taxation — the only missing Tier 1 items. The revision and mock phases can still proceed, targeting a passable Tier 1 score.

───

Scenario B: One major topic requires substantially more time than estimated (e.g., Final Accounts needs 8 h instead of 4 h).

· Mitigation built in: The Consolidation phase (6 days) contains flexible practice time. If Final Accounts overruns, the student can shift 2 days from Tier 2 completion in Consolidation to extra Final Accounts drill. Partnership Accounts can be scoped even leaner (only admission entries).
· Structural safety: The Tier 1 core remains intact because the overrun topic is itself Tier 1; the plan sacrifices Tier 2 depth, not Tier 1 breadth.

───

Scenario C: Student progresses faster than expected (e.g., completes Core by Day 10).

· Opportunity: Expand Consolidation phase by 4 additional days. Increase mock count from 2 to 4, and add deeper practice on Partnership and Cost Accounting. The extra time is used for higher‑order application, not for early termination.

───

Scenario D: Exam confirmed for a horizon meaningfully shorter than 1 month (e.g., 3 weeks from now).

· Tier 1 subplan activation:
· Phase 1 (Foundation): Compressed to 3 days (skip Voucher Approach, reduce Journal practice to essential).
· Phase 2 (Core): Run for 8 days covering only Tier 1 accountancy (up to Final Accounts, plus basic Partnership and Taxation), Tier 1 GK, English, Science, Computers, and the critical math/econ topics. Tier 2 entirely dropped.
· Phase 3 (Consolidation): 3 days — intensive Tier 1 mixed practice.
· Phase 4 (Revision): 2 days — sweep of Tier 1 only.
· Phases 5–7: 2 days — 1 timed mock + final revision.
· Total: 3+8+3+2+2 = 18 days, fitting within 3 weeks. This compressed Tier 1 subplan still covers all Critical + High topics and can yield a competitive score.

───

All scenarios confirm that the roadmap is robust: Tier 1 alone, across all phases, forms a coherent and exam‑ready subplan — exactly as designed in Stage 1’s Priority Matrix.

───

Stage 2 Summary
The 28‑day Roadmap is structured in seven phases — Foundation, Core Learning, Consolidation, First Revision, PYQs & Mock Tests, Weak‑Area Improvement, and Final Revision — with continuous revision embedded throughout. Accountancy builds in a strict procedural chain, Economics follows a micro‑to‑macro flow, and other subjects are interleaved daily for retrieval practice. Three timed mocks and two untimed PYQs are placed in the final week, preceded by cumulative quizzes and blank‑page retrieval. The plan can be compressed to a ~3‑week Tier 1‑only version if the exam is suddenly confirmed earlier, without sacrificing a coherent, pass‑ready knowledge base.
