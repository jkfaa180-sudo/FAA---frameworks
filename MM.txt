Apportionment basis for topic-level weightage (used once, applied to every subject): I assign each topic a share of its subject's official marks in proportion to (conceptual complexity × prerequisite density × required practice intensity × topic breadth), where each factor is rated 1–5 against the rest of the topics in that same subject. The four ratings are multiplied to give a raw weight; each topic's share = its raw weight ÷ the sum of raw weights for that subject × the subject's official marks. This makes heavier/more-broad/more-practice-intensive topics carry proportionally more questions — which is what determines actual exam score weight.

2. Learning Dependency Map (Revised)
This is constructed before the sequence and is the single source of truth for ordering. The syllabus order is ignored here on purpose — it interleaves a beginner's logical learning path with unrelated topics.
Revisions vs. the first draft of this map (post-friend-feedback):
▪ Partnership moved from "teach last" to immediately after Final Accounts (the highest-difficulty topic needs P&L/BS scaffolding to be fresh).
▪ Mathematics reordered: Linear Equations first (quick wins) → Set/Relations/Functions → Matrices (drill confidence) → P&C (conceptual leap after drill) → Probability → Coord Geo/Vectors → Limits → Derivatives.
▪ Statistics reordered: data-collection cluster compressed → Central Tendency → Demography (warm-up) → Probability (waits for Math P&C) → Index Numbers → Attributes → Vital Statistics.
▪ Economics split-the-difference: Micro core (Demand → IC → Market Pricing) first, then National Income + Growth, then Fiscal/Monetary/RBI pulled forward to anchor the Accountancy PFMS cluster, then Production/Factor Pricing, then Reforms/Developing/Planning filler at end.
▪ Computers explicit chain: Basic Apps & I/O → Hardware → Software → OS → MS Office tools → Virus → Open Source → IT-in-Gov.
▪ GK linearised to 14 clusters: India general first → J&K cluster (History → Geography → Economy → Culture → Tourism) → J&K Reorganisation Act → Current Affairs as final-week digest, not a study topic.
▪ Accountancy tail (PFMS/IFMS/Taxation/Social/Audit/Cost/Budgetary/Developments) explicitly grouped as a "conceptual/factual tail" — low internal dependency, batch-studied as parallel short blocks within one phase.

[20]  Developments in Accounting                              — awareness; smallest

Why Partnership moved to right after BS: Partnership sub-cases (admission, retirement, dissolution, profit-sharing ratios, goodwill) re-use Trading/P&L/BS mechanics — capital adjustments, accumulated profits, drawings, current accounts. If Partnership is taught 2+ weeks after Final Accounts, the student must rebuild that scaffolding. The friend's sequencing is correct for a beginner.
Why BRS is in the tail, not the procedural chain: BRS logic depends only on Cash Book + conceptual understanding of timing differences. It can be taught anytime after Cash Book and doesn't depend on Final Accounts or Partnership. Putting it in the conceptual/factual tail clusters it with low-internal-dependency topics.
Why the "tail" is batched: Items [11]–[19] share a property — none depends on any other within this set, and none depends on Partnership either. They can run as parallel short blocks within one phase, cutting switching costs. (Bank Reconciliation is the partial exception; it's slightly more reasoning-heavy, so it gets a slightly longer block — see Stage 2.)

4. Recommended Preparation Duration + Timeline Justification
Total study hours required (leanest sufficient estimate): ~197 hours.
Daily capacity: 4–5 hours/day as specified.
Math: 197 ÷ 4.5 hr/day ≈ 44 days (~6.3 weeks).
Vs. 1-month working horizon (30 days): The leanest sufficient total exceeds 30 days of full study. This is a genuine mismatch that must not be silently compressed. Two facts follow:
1. Tier 1 alone (see §5) is ~140 hours, which at 4.5 hr/day = ~31 days — a single full month. So Tier 1 fits the working horizon if the student runs at full capacity every day. With normal life frictions, this is the realistic Tier-1 scope.
2. Tier 2 (the remaining ~57 hours) is the buffer. It cannot fit in 30 days without compressing Tier 1 to the point of risk. Per the Exam Timeline Assumption, the 1-month lean scoping governs Tier 1+2 together; this plan therefore presents a 6-week full-syllabus plan as the primary deliverable, with an explicit Tier-1-only 30-day sub-plan visible throughout so the user can collapse to it if the exam is confirmed inside the working horizon.
Final recommended duration (leanest sufficient): 6 weeks = 42 days = ~190–197 study hours, built around a Tier-1 30-day sub-plan.
If the exam is later confirmed for a longer horizon, add hours to Tier 2 (mostly additional mock tests, more revision cycles, and depth on Cost/Partnership/Single-entry — see Stage 4 reconciliation table). Don't rebuild the plan.
This does not violate the Planning Principle (duration was derived from the topic-hour table, not reverse-engineered from "6 weeks"); the 1-month vs. 6-week reconciliation is a consequence of the lean total exceeding the working horizon, surfaced explicitly per the Exam Timeline Assumption rule.

3. Revision Strategy (continuous from Phase 1)

Method
Where it's used in this plan
Frequency

Active Recall
Every day's session ends with 10-min "write what you remember" from the day's topic. Phase 4 and Phase 7 are full-day active-recall sessions.
Daily from Day 1

Retrieval Practice
Phase 5 onward: every MCQ attempted is an act of retrieval. After Phase 5, every missed MCQ is re-attempted the next day (spaced retrieval).
Daily from Day 26

Spaced Repetition
Error log items are re-tested on Day +1, Day +3, Day +7, Day +14, Day +21 (expanding intervals). Critical formulas (debit/credit rules, BS equation, ratio formulas, P&C formulas, limits/derivatives rules, central tendency formulas) are rehearsed on Day +1, Day +3, Day +7, then weekly.
Built into Phase 4–7

Interleaving
Phase 4 (First Revision) and Phase 7 (Final Revision) deliberately mix subjects and topics in a single session. Mocks (Phase 5–6) are inherently interleaved.
Phases 4, 5, 6, 7

Deliberate Practice
All "Practice" hours in Stage 1's T/P/R/M split count as deliberate practice: identifying the weakest sub-topic and drilling it with feedback (error log).
Embedded in every topic

Feynman Technique
After each topic, the student explains it in 2 minutes out loud (or writes it as if teaching). Used heavily in Phase 1 (foundations) and Phase 4 (revision).
After every new topic

Error Analysis
A running error log is maintained from Day 1. Every wrong answer (MCQ or problem) is logged with: topic, sub-topic, type of error (conceptual / careless / time-pressure / formula-misuse), and the corrected version. Reviewed in Phase 4 + Phase 7.
Daily from Day 1

Progressive Difficulty
Topic sequencing in the dependency map (Stage 1 §2) is the difficulty progression: foundations → procedural → conceptual. Within each topic, practice starts with worked examples, moves to guided problems, then to MCQ bank problems.
Embedded in every topic


Why each method appears where it does:
• Active Recall is embedded from Day 1 because delayed start of recall practice is one of the highest-cost mistakes in self-study.
• Spaced Repetition starts becoming valuable from Phase 4 onward (when there are enough items to space).
• Interleaving is concentrated in revision phases because by Phase 4 the student has enough topics to interleave.
• Feynman is heaviest in Phase 1 because foundations need the deepest encoding; later phases use it lightly.
