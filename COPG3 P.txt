PART A — Sequencing Rationale (why dependency-driven, not syllabus order)

The syllabus lists subjects 1→8 (GK first, Accountancy second, etc.). I reject that as the study order for three structural reasons, and weigh the main alternative before confirming.

The decisive sequencing decisions

Decision 1 — Accountancy starts Day 1, not after GK.
• Syllabus order puts GK first. Dependency map shows Accountancy has the only deep prerequisite chain (8 links deep) and the largest hour load (83h) . A skill chain that long must start earliest to allow distributed practice and spaced revision [LS: spacing effect]. Starting it late forces last-minute cramming of the exact skill that can't be crammed.
• Cognitive-continuity gain: building debit/credit → journal → ledger → trial balance → final accounts as one unbroken conceptual thread maximizes transfer; interrupting it costs re-warm-up time.

Decision 2 — Recall subjects (Computers, Science, English, GK static, CA) run in parallel from Day 1, not in sequence.
• These have near-zero prerequisites (Stage 1 §2C), so nothing is gained by sequencing them. Running a daily recall block alongside the skill block exploits interleaving[LS] — alternating a procedural subject (Accountancy) with declarative recall (Science/Computers) reduces fatigue and improves retention vs. massing one subject.
• Negative-marking link: parallel daily drip of high-marks-per-hour recall subjects banks "safe" marks early, reducing pressure to guess later.

Decision 3 — Demography is taught in the Statistics slot and reused in GK, never twice.
• It appears in both subjects officially. Teaching the formula cluster once (CBR/CDR/GFR/SFR/GRR/NRR) and tagging GK's demography line as "covered" saves ~3–4h with zero coverage loss.

Credible alternative weighed: strict weightage-first ordering
A pure weightage-first sequence would study GK (30) then Accountancy (30) then the 10-mark subjects by descending marks-per-hour.
• Why it's weaker: It ignores prerequisite time-distribution. Accountancy's 30 marks aren't won by studying it first in a block — they're won by studying it longest, with spacing. Weightage-first would either (a) front-load GK and delay the un-crammable skill chain, or (b) block Accountancy into one massed stretch, killing the spacing effect. It also wastes the free interleaving available from parallel recall subjects.
• What I borrow from it: weightage-first correctly says high-marks-per-hour subjects (Computers, Science) deserve early, reliable inclusion — so I run them in the parallel recall track from Week 1 rather than deferring them. This captures the alternative's one real strength without its prerequisite blindness.

Confirmed order: a two-track parallel design — a Skill Track (Accountancy spine, then the Math/Stats quantitative chain) and a Recall Track (Computers, Science, English, GK, Economics, CA drip) — synchronized phase by phase below.

PART C — Revision Strategy [LS]

Revision is continuous from Phase 1, not a late phase only:
• Daily: 20–30 min retrieval of yesterday's material (active recall, closed-book) — start of every study day.
• Weekly: end-of-week mixed quiz across that week's topics (interleaving).
• Spaced repetition schedule for recall decks: review at Day 1 → 3 → 7 → 14 → before exam (expanding intervals).
• Three full sweeps total: continuous daily (built-in) + structured First Revision (Phase 4) + Final Revision (Phase 8).
• Feynman checkpoints: for the 5 hardest Accountancy topics, write a plain-language explanation; gaps in the explanation reveal gaps in understanding.

PART D — Practice Strategy [LS: progressive difficulty, deliberate practice]

• Progression gate: never attempt application above the level taught — e.g., no multi-adjustment Final Accounts before single-entry Final Accounts is solid.
• Volume targets [INF]: ~8–10 problems per Accountancy skill topic in Phase 2; rising to mixed/timed sets in Phase 5–6.
• Error log is the spine: every wrong answer logged with cause tag (concept gap / careless / misread / formula). Phase 7 attacks the top cause-tags.
• Negative-marking practice: from Phase 5, every practice question gets an explicit attempt/skip decision; track decision-accuracy, not just answer-accuracy.

PART E — Mock Test Strategy [LS: testing effect]

• Type progression: topic quizzes (Phase 2–4) → subject mini-mocks (per Stage 1 mock hours) → PYQ sets (Phase 5) → full 120-Q timed mocks (Phase 6) .
• Escalating frequency toward exam (QC #9): spaced early, near-daily in the final stretch, each followed by error analysis ≥ the mock's own duration initially.
• Penalty calibration: after each mock, compute gross vs. net score to tune the personal skip threshold (how many options must be eliminable before attempting).
