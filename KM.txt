1. Learning Dependency Map (Revised)

Accountancy (Strict Sequential Core)
Introduction to Financial Accounting & terms → Elements of Double Entry Book Keeping & Accounting Equation → Journal → Ledger Accounts → Cash Book → Bank Reconciliation Statement → Trial Balance → Trading Account → Profit & Loss Account → Balance Sheet → Partnership Accounts → Financial Management / Statements → Cost Accounting → Cost Management–Budgetary Control → Financial Audit → Voucher Approach in Accounting → Social Accounting, Social Audit & Cash-based single entry → PFMS → Indian Financial Management System → Taxation, Tax Laws Direct & Indirect → Developments in Accounting.

Rationale for revisions: Cash Book is placed before Bank Reconciliation (standard pedagogical flow: record cash transactions, then reconcile with bank statement). The "conceptual/factual tail" (Voucher Approach through Developments) is batched after the procedural core and financial audit, since these topics require only basic accounting literacy and have low internal dependency. Voucher Approach is placed after the core commercial cycle is complete, so the student does not conflate government voucher accounting with commercial double entry before anchoring the latter.

Mathematics (Two chains + independents)
• Chain A: Linear Equations with two variables → Set Theory Basic Concepts and Applications → Relations and Functions → Limits & Derivatives
• Chain B: Matrices & Determinants
• Bridge: Permutations & Combinations & Binomial Theorem → Probability (shared with Statistics)
• Independent: Simple/Compound Interest; Coordinate & Three dimensional Geometry

Rationale for revisions: Linear Equations (concrete, familiar) now opens the sequence instead of Set Theory, reducing initial cognitive load for a beginner. Permutations/Combinations feeds Probability, which in turn supports Statistics.

Statistics (One main chain)
Data Collection / Tabulation / Questionnaires → Measures of Central Tendency → Demography–Census, its features and functions → Probability (after Mathematics Permutations/Combinations) → Theory of Index Numbers → Vital Statistics → Theory of Attributes.

Rationale for revisions: Demography is placed before Probability as a gentle transition from descriptive to inferential thinking. Data-collection topics are batched at the start.

Economics (Three chains)
• Chain A: Introduction of Economics → Demand Analysis → Theory of consumers demand using indifference curve technique → Factors of production & Laws of Production → Factor Pricing – Marginal productivity Theory & Ricardian Theory of Rent → Pricing under various forms of Market
• Chain B: Concept & Measurement of National Income → Concept of Economic Growth and its measurement
• Chain C: Fiscal & Monetary Policy → Role of RBI
• Independent: Characteristics and problems of developing economy → Planning vs Market Economy → Economic Reforms in India

Rationale for revisions: The micro sequence (Demand → Consumer Behaviour → Production → Factor Pricing → Market Structures) is now fully ordered by conceptual dependency. The macro tail forms a single readable narrative.

Cross-Subject Links
• Basic arithmetic (Interest, Linear Equations) supports Economics calculations.
• Core Accountancy literacy supports Financial Management, PFMS, and some Economic terminology.
• English comprehension is a passive enabler for all subjects, not a hard prerequisite.
• Mathematics Probability feeds Statistics Probability.
• GK Demography–Census cross-references Statistics Demography.
• GK Environment/Ecology cross-references General Science Environment.

Independent / Low-Dependency Clusters
• General Knowledge (all topics factual; J&K Reorganisation Act is isolated legal content; Current Events compiled at the end).
• General Science (topic-level independence at this depth).
• Computers (linear hardware → software → OS → applications flow; see below).
• General English (grammar topics independent; comprehension integrates them).

Computers (Linear flow)
Basic Applications of Computer and its component / Fundamentals of computer sciences → Input & output Devices → Hardware & Software, Elements of Computers Processing System → Hardware, CPU, Peripherals, Storage Media, Software Definition, Role and Categories, Firmware and Human-ware → Operating system → Knowledge of MS Word, MS Excel, MS Access, MS Powerpoint, PDF Internet and E-mail → Concept of Computer Virus and Anti-Virus → Concept of Open Source Technologies → Role of Information Technology in Governance.

General Knowledge (Structured block sequence)
Political & Physical divisions of World & India → Indian Culture, Heritage and Freedom Struggle/Movement → Demography–Census, its features and functions → Transport & Communication → Environment, Ecology & Bio-diversity → J&K UT History → J&K UT Geography → J&K UT Economy → J&K UT Heritage & Culture → J&K UT Important Tourist Destinations → J&K Reorganisation Act → Current Events of National and International importance.

Sequencing implication: Accountancy core must start immediately and proceed in strict order. Mathematics Chain A should start early because it feeds Statistics Probability. Economics can begin once basic math comfort is established. GK, Science, and Computers are distributed throughout as cognitive-load balancers.

1. Apportionment Basis for Topic-Level Weightage

The syllabus assigns marks only at the subject level; no official topic-level weightage exists. The "Derived Wt (Inferred)" figures in the table below are analytical apportionments produced by:
1. Starting from the subject's official marks;
2. Dividing by the number of topics to obtain a baseline;
3. Adjusting each topic's share upward or downward based on the same four factors used for hour estimation: conceptual complexity, prerequisite density, required practice intensity, and topic breadth within the subject.

Topics that are conceptually complex, prerequisite-heavy, practice-intensive, or broad receive a higher share; narrow, factual, or low-practice topics receive a lower share. The sum of inferred weights across all topics in a subject equals the subject's official marks. These figures are not official syllabus data and are explicitly labeled as inferred.

1. Recommended Preparation Duration + Timeline Justification

Recommended Preparation Duration: Approximately 7–9 weeks (49–62 days) at a sustainable 4–5 hours/day.

Calculation:
• Total estimated study load: 246.5 hours
• At 4 hours/day: 61.6 days (8.8 weeks)
• At 5 hours/day: 49.3 days (7.0 weeks)

Timeline Justification:
1. Why this exceeds the 1-month working horizon: The syllabus spans 120 marks across 8 subjects with 97 distinct topics. Accountancy alone (30 marks) requires building a 10-topic procedural chain from absolute zero; even with lean scoping it consumes 73.5 hours. The two 30-mark subjects together require 120 hours—more than one month at 4–5 hours/day. Compressing total workload into 30 days would require either exceeding the 5-hour daily safety ceiling or dropping entire subjects, both of which violate the optimization hierarchy (expected score and retention outrank speed).
2. Tier 1 minimal readiness: The Critical + High priority subset (Tier 1) requires 172.5 hours. At 5 hours/day this is 35 days (5 weeks); at 4 hours/day it is 43 days (6 weeks). Therefore, even the leanest coherent exam-ready subplan slightly exceeds a 1-month horizon at the stated daily capacity. If the exam is confirmed within 1 month, the student must either increase daily hours beyond 5 or accept coverage of only Accountancy + GK core plus scattered high-yield topics from other subjects.
3. Pedagogical floor: The estimates already reflect the leanest sufficient version—practice intensity is set at the minimum threshold for retention in an objective exam, and revision is integrated rather than deferred. Further compression would sacrifice long-term retention (priority #5) and expected examination score (priority #3).

Phase-wise Roadmap

Phase 1: Foundation (Days 1–10)
Objective: Build the prerequisite competencies for all downstream subjects. Establish accounting literacy from absolute zero, basic mathematical maturity, and factual baselines for GK, Science, and Computers.

Duration: 10 days (4.5 hrs/day = 45 hrs total).

Topics & Tier Marking:
• Accountancy [Tier 1 Critical]: Introduction to Financial Accounting & terms; Elements of Double Entry Book Keeping; Accounting Equation & Journal; Ledger Accounts.
• Mathematics [Tier 1 High]: Linear Equations with two variables; Set Theory Basic Concepts and Applications.
• General Knowledge [Tier 1 High]: Political & Physical divisions of World & India; Demography–Census; Indian Geography (physical divisions).
• General English [Tier 1 High]: Tenses; Articles; Modals.
• Knowledge of Computers [Tier 1 High]: Fundamentals of computer sciences; Input & output Devices; Hardware & Software elements; Hardware, CPU, Peripherals, Storage Media.
• General Science [Tier 1 High]: Environment, Ecology & Bio-diversity; Ecosystem – components, Food chains and Food webs.

Intensity: High. New concept density is maximal here; the student has no prior accounting schema to anchor to.

Deliverables: Can journalize 20 basic transactions independently, post to ledger, and extract a trial balance. Can solve linear equations and set-theory Venn problems. Can recall constitutional structure and 2011 Census highlights.

Exit Criteria: ≥80% accuracy on basic Accountancy drill (journal/ledger). Zero unresolved errors in English grammar baseline.

Learning Methods: Feynman Technique for accounting terms (explain "Why is capital a liability?" in simple words); Error Analysis log initiated; daily 15-min Active Recall of previous day's accounting rules before starting new material.

───

Phase 2: Core Learning (Days 11–24)
Objective: Complete the Accountancy procedural core through Final Accounts; advance Mathematics through combinatorics; begin Statistics and Economics micro foundations.

Duration: 14 days (4.5 hrs/day = 63 hrs total).

Topics & Tier Marking:
• Accountancy [Tier 1 Critical]: Cash Book; Bank Reconciliation Statement; Trial Balance; Trading Account; Profit & Loss Account and Balance Sheet; Financial Audit.
• Mathematics [Tier 1 High]: Relations and Functions; Matrices & Determinants; Permutations/Combinations & Binomial Theorem.
• Statistics [Tier 1 High]: Primary and secondary data; Methods of collecting data; Preparation of questionnaires; Tabulation and compilation; Measures of central Tendency.
• General Knowledge [Tier 1 High]: Indian Culture, Heritage and Freedom Struggle; Transport & Communication; Important Rivers & Lakes.
• General English [Tier 1 High]: Narration; Active & Passive Voice; Rearranging of jumbled sentences.
• Knowledge of Computers [Tier 1 High]: Operating system; Knowledge of MS Word, MS Excel, MS Access, MS Powerpoint, PDF Internet and E-mail.
• General Science [Tier 1 High]: Newtons Laws of Motion; Various sources of energy (conventional & non-conventional).

Intensity: Very High. Profit & Loss Account & Balance Sheet is the steepest learning curve in the entire syllabus.

Deliverables: Can prepare complete Final Accounts with 5 standard adjustments (outstanding expenses, prepaid, depreciation, bad debts, closing stock). Can solve permutation/combination problems involving restricted positions. Can explain demand determinants.

Exit Criteria: Correctly prepare 3 full Final Accounts independently in under 45 minutes each. Score ≥75% on Statistics central tendency problems.

Learning Methods: Deliberate Practice for Accountancy adjustments (isolate each adjustment type, master individually, then interleave); Retrieval Practice via daily 10-min quiz on previous day's accounting formats; Progressive Difficulty in Math (standard nCr → restricted positions → binomial expansion).

───

Phase 3: Consolidation (Days 25–38)
Objective: Master advanced Accountancy (Partnership, Cost, Taxation); complete Mathematics and Statistics; cover Economics macro and market structures; cover J&K-specific GK and remaining subjects.

Major Sequencing Decisions & Rationale

Decision 1: Accountancy core begins on Day 1 and remains the primary thread through Phase 1–3.
• Alternative: A weightage-first sequence might start with GK (also 30 marks) because it is factual and easier.
• Why dependency-driven is superior: GK is largely independent and can be safely studied in parallel. Accountancy has a 10-topic procedural chain where each topic depends on the previous. Delaying Accountancy would compress the most difficult subject into the final weeks, leaving no buffer for the steep learning curve at P&L/Balance Sheet and Partnership. Starting immediately maximizes practice time and allows error analysis to accumulate. The weightage-first alternative would produce a student who knows Indian polity but cannot journalize a transaction—an incoherent exam state.

Decision 2: Mathematics Permutations/Combinations precedes Statistics Probability.
• Alternative: Teach Statistics Probability independently using counting principles taught within the Statistics section.
• Why dependency-driven is superior: The syllabus lists Probability under both Mathematics and Statistics. The mathematical treatment of counting (nCr, nPr, binomial coefficients) is deeper in the Math section. Learning it in Math first provides the combinatorial toolkit that makes Statistics Probability problems (conditional probability, expectation) solvable without confusion. The alternative would require teaching counting twice or treating Statistics Probability as rote memorization, which degrades retention and transfer.

Decision 3: Economics micro chain (Demand → Consumer → Production → Factor Pricing → Market Forms) precedes macro (National Income → Growth → Fiscal/Monetary).
• Alternative: Teach macro first (National Income, Growth) because these are high-yield and frequently asked.
• Why dependency-driven is superior: National Income measurement requires understanding factor payments (rent, wages, interest, profit), which are explained in Factor Pricing. Market structure determines how factor prices are set. Without the micro foundation, macro becomes rote memorization of formulas (GDP = C+I+G+NX) without economic intuition. The dependency-driven order builds conceptual continuity; the alternative sacrifices understanding for perceived exam frequency and increases the risk of formula confusion under exam pressure.

Decision 4: J&K Reorganisation Act is placed at the end of the J&K GK block, not immediately after Indian Polity.
• Alternative: Teach the Reorganisation Act immediately after Indian Polity because it is legal/constitutional in nature.
• Why dependency-driven is superior: The Reorganisation Act restructured J&K from a state into a UT, altering its political, geographic, and administrative boundaries. Understanding the Act requires prior knowledge of what J&K was before (History, Geography, Economy) to appreciate what changed. Studying it after the J&K block provides context and makes the legal provisions meaningful rather than abstract memorization.

Decision 5: English Comprehension is delayed until Phase 3, after discrete grammar mastery.
• Alternative: Start with Comprehension because it is the highest-inferred-weight topic in English (2.5 marks) and integrates all skills.
• Why dependency-driven is superior: Comprehension requires application of tense consistency, article usage, modal meaning, narration understanding, and active/passive recognition. A beginner attempting comprehension before discrete grammar mastery will guess rather than apply rules, reinforcing errors. The dependency-driven order builds the toolkit first; comprehension becomes an integrative capstone rather than a source of confusion.

Revision Strategy

Revision is continuous, not terminal.

• Daily (embedded in every phase): 15–20 min Active Recall at the start of each session. Student closes notes and reproduces the previous day's core concept (e.g., "Write the 6 types of journal entries from memory").
• Weekly (every 7th day): 1-hour consolidation. Review the week's error log; re-solve 5 problems from the weakest topic of the week.
• Phase 4 (First Revision): Full-syllabus blank-page reproduction. Student writes all Accountancy formats, all Math formulas, and all Economics definitions from memory without reference.
• Phase 8 (Final Revision): Rapid flashcard pass of Tier 1 topics only. No new problem sets.

───

Practice Strategy

• Theory:Practice ratio: Accountancy 1:1 (every hour of theory is matched with an hour of problems); GK 2:1 (more reading, less drill); Math/Stats 1:1; Economics 3:2.
• Deliberate Practice blocks: 45-minute focused problem sets followed by 15-minute error analysis. No passive re-reading of solutions.
• Progressive Difficulty: Within each topic, problems are sequenced Easy → Medium → Hard. A topic is not considered "exited" until the student solves 3 consecutive Hard problems correctly.
• Error Log: Every incorrect problem is logged with (1) error type, (2) prerequisite gap if any, (3) correct solution written independently after 24 hours.

Stress Test Results

Before finalizing, the roadmap was tested against four scenarios:

(a) Student misses one week (Days 15–21): Would have completed Foundation (Terms through Ledger) but missed Cash Book through Trading Account. The dependency chain is broken. Repair: The student must complete Cash Book → BRS → Trial Balance → Trading Account before entering Phase 3. This pushes Phase 3 back by 5 days. Because Phase 3 is 14 days and the total frame has buffer, this is manageable if the student maintains 5 hrs/day during catch-up. The structural resilience comes from the explicit dependency chain.

(b) One major topic requires substantially more time (e.g., P&L/Balance Sheet needs 10 hrs instead of 7.5): Adds 2.5 hrs. Repair: Absorbed by compressing Tier 2 topics (Developments in Accounting, 1 hr; Open Source, 1 hr; J&K Tourism, 0.5 hr). Tier 1 integrity is preserved.

(c) Student progresses faster than expected (finishes Phase 1 in 8 days): Gains 2 days. Repair: Options: (i) extra deliberate practice on Journal/Ledger, (ii) start Phase 2 early, or (iii) bank as rest days. The modular phase structure allows any of these.

(d) Exam confirmed for a horizon meaningfully shorter than 1 month (e.g., 3 weeks): Tier 1 subplan verification: Tier 1 topics across Phases 1–3 cover all 8 subjects and constitute a coherent exam-ready plan. The student would execute Phase 1–3 (skipping Tier 2 topics within those phases), then jump to Phase 6–8 (2 mocks + final revision). This is minimal but exam-ready. The plan does not depend on Tier 2 for coherence.

Phase structure summary: The roadmap runs 8 phases over approximately 8 weeks (62 days at 4 hrs/day, or 50 days at 5 hrs/day). Accountancy and GK are front-loaded and interleaved with lighter subjects to manage cognitive load. Revision is embedded from Day 1, mock frequency escalates from Phase 5 onward, and Tier 1 topics form a self-contained exam-ready subplan that can be extracted if the timeline collapses.
